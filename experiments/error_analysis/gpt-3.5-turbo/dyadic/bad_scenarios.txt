The immense variety of scenarios can be distilled into several common themes:

1. **Ethical Dilemmas Involving Sacrifice for the Greater Good:** A significant number of scenarios involve ethical dilemmas where the choice is between letting one person die or saving more lives. These scenarios reflect the classic "trolley problem" in moral philosophy, highlighting decisions where an individual must actively choose to sacrifice one person to save others. Examples include the scenario of the helicopter pilot pushing someone out to save others ("...the helicopter continues to fall and risks crashing into a rock face...") and diverting a train to a track with one person to save five workers ("...the locomotive will continue its course and end up running into five men...").

2. **Bending or Breaking Rules for Personal Gain:** Several scenarios involve an individual breaking rules or laws for personal benefit. These include actions such as falsifying a CV to gain employment ("...you insert false information in your CV about your ability in information technology.") and making false tax declarations to save a business ("...you will reduce the tax to pay and the company’s balance sheet will not go into the red.").

3. **Acts of Dishonesty or Deception:** Many scenarios involve scenarios where lying, stealing, or similar deceptive actions occurred. These could stem from trying to avoid consequences or to gain an advantage. For instance, someone keeping money from a cashier's mistake ("...has given you €30 too much change.") and not revealing illegal activities for personal safety or financial gain ("...there was a partner that was providing a great deal of money by importing marijuana...").

4. **Moral Judgments and Prejudices:** Some scenarios focus on personal or societal judgments about others' behaviors or beliefs, highlighting bias and prejudice. These scenarios explore topics like disrespect towards national symbols or authorities ("...burned their country's flag because they don't like their country.") and critiquing perceived moral failings such as refusing to attend a relative's funeral ("Refused to attend the funeral of a relative he disliked").

5. **Everyday Human Interactions and Mistakes:** Several scenarios depict ordinary activities, errors, or benign actions, often devoid of moral complexity. These involve situations like buying a favorite dish ("Person X ordered their favorite dish from a Chinese restaurant.") or accidentally breaking eggs while grocery shopping ("Person X brought in the groceries...causing the eggs to break.").

These categories encapsulate how humans face moral decisions, interactions, and the consequences of their actions in diverse situations, ranging from the mundane to the morally challenging.

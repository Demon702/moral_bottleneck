The scenarios can be grouped based on several common themes, which are explained below.

1. **Moral Dilemmas and Utilitarian Decisions:** Many scenarios involve difficult moral decisions where one person's life is sacrificed to save many others. These scenarios often highlight utilitarian principles, where the greatest good for the greatest number is prioritized. Examples include pushing someone off a chairlift to save others ("You push the person next to you off the chair to lighten the load..."), and diverting a train to a track with one person to save five others ("You throw this person off the bridge...").

2. **Lying and Deception for Personal Gain:** Several scenarios involve deception or lying to gain personal benefits or avoid negative consequences. This includes pretending to be in the actor's union ("So I would pretend I was in the union.") and lying on a CV to secure a job ("You insert false information in your CV...").

3. **Neglecting Responsibilities or Being Exploitative:** Scenarios where individuals neglect their responsibilities or exploit others are common. An example is not paying back money lent by a friend ("Lent my friend a large amount of money...") and not contributing to a mother's economic stability ("Instead of contributing...I traveled.").

4. **Acts of Violence or Coercion:** Some scenarios involve explicit acts of violence or coercion, sometimes to achieve a strategic goal. For instance, breaking a combatant's hand for information ("Breaks the hand of a wounded combatant...") and threatening to kill a farmer ("Threatens to kill a farmer to gain information.").

5. **Everyday Life and Mistakes:** There are mundane scenarios that depict everyday activities, sometimes involving minor mistakes or lapses in judgment. Examples include forgetting to turn off the stove ("Person X forgot to turn the stove off...") and accidentally knocking a glass off a table ("Person X accidentally knocked a glass off...").

6. **Judgment Calls in Crisis Situations:** A number of scenarios involve making difficult judgment calls during crisis situations where the outcome is uncertain. For instance, pushing a person into flames to prevent a fire from spreading to a car ("You push a passer-by onto the flames..."). These scenarios emphasize the complexity of split-second decision-making under pressure.

These themes highlight different aspects of human behavior and decision-making, covering moral complexity, personal integrity, responsibility, and the challenging nature of crisis decision-making.

The scenarios presented can be grouped into several common themes based on the actions and moral implications involved. Here are some of the themes identified:

1. **Sacrificing One for Many**: Several scenarios involve making a difficult decision to sacrifice one person to save multiple others. This is a classic ethical dilemma often referred to as the "trolley problem." Examples include pushing a person out of a helicopter to save others and diverting a trolley to a track with one worker to save five others.

2. **Deception and Dishonesty**: Many scenarios involve lying, cheating, or deceiving others for personal gain or to avoid negative consequences. This includes actions like lying about one's age to get into dance school, cheating on a partner, or falsifying a CV to get a job. An example is the scenario where someone lies about being in a union to receive better treatment.

3. **Acts of Kindness and Altruism**: Some scenarios depict selfless acts intended to help others without expecting anything in return. These include volunteering at a shelter, helping a neighbor fix a fence, or visiting a sick friend in the hospital. An example is the person who volunteers to teach English to newly arrived immigrants.

4. **Selfishness and Harmful Actions**: There are scenarios where individuals act out of selfishness or malice, causing harm to others. This includes actions like embezzling money from a charity, intentionally swerving to hit a squirrel, or putting broken glass in children's cereal packets. An example is the person who embezzled money to feed their gambling habit.

5. **Moral Courage and Ethical Decisions**: Some scenarios involve making difficult ethical decisions that require moral courage, such as calling off an airstrike due to the presence of civilians or risking one's life to save others. An example is the person who risked their life to rescue an animal trapped in a burning house.

6. **Everyday Ethical Dilemmas**: These scenarios involve common situations where individuals face moral choices, such as deciding whether to return extra change given by a cashier or whether to tell a friend about mean comments made behind their back. An example is the person who decided to return the extra change given by a cashier.

7. **Social and Cultural Norms**: Some scenarios highlight actions that challenge or conform to social and cultural norms, such as refusing to stand for a judge or joining the military to experience killing. An example is the person who joined the military despite having a religious opt-out.

These themes reflect a wide range of human behaviors and ethical considerations, illustrating the complexity of moral decision-making in various contexts.
